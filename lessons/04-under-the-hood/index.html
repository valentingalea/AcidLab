Section 1How a real oscillator works — a leaky bucket

In silicon, a sawtooth isn't a formula — it's a capacitor charging up and dumping, over and over. A steady current trickles into a capacitor; its voltage ramps up linearly; when it hits a threshold, a transistor shorts it to zero in an instant — then it starts again. That rising ramp with the sudden drop is the sawtooth. Fill the bucket, tip it out, repeat.

Watch it: the dot is the capacitor's voltage. The charge current slider is the pitch — more current fills the bucket faster, so it dumps more often = a higher note.

Voltage control & the exp converter. How does a voltage from a key become that charge current? Through an exponential converter — because pitch is exponential (music is 1 volt = 1 octave, i.e. double the frequency), and a transistor's current-vs-voltage curve is conveniently exponential. That's the "V/oct" standard Roland, Moog and Eurorack all share.

// the analog tax: that exponential converter drifts with temperature — the single biggest reason old synths go out of tune as they warm up (fixed with a "tempco" resistor). And an analog ramp is never band-limited — its harmonics run past the ear with no anti-aliasing, which is part of the "warmth". Our digital OscillatorNode is a clean, drift-free, band-limited idealisation of exactly this circuit.

The TB-303's signal path is almost the minimal synth, and it's all analog:

The filter is the legend, and it's a genuine oddity. It's a 4-pole ladder — but built from transistors wired to act as diodes (base and collector shorted), so it's a diode ladder, not the buffered transistor ladder of a Moog. Two consequences make the 303 sound like the 303:

quirkwhy it matters
the poles interact electrically (no buffers between them)they're not clean, isolated stages — the messy coupling is a big part of the dirty, squelchy tone
it measures more like 18 dB/oct, not a clean 24a "flawed" 4-pole that behaves like ~3 poles — gentler and more vocal than a Moog. (People argue about the exact number to this day.)
tuned to stop just short of self-oscillationa Moog sings on its own at max resonance; the stock 303 deliberately doesn't — its scream is resonance right at the edge. (The famous "Devil Fish" mod pushes it over.)

Our lessons 02–03 faked this filter with two BiquadFilterNodes (a clean 24 dB/oct). That's ~90% of the sound for almost no code — but it can't do the last, best 10%: the interacting-pole grit, and a filter that becomes an oscillator. For that we have to compute the ladder ourselves.

Section 4When a filter becomes an oscillator

Here's the beautiful part. A resonant filter has a feedback loop: some output is fed back to the input. Turn that feedback up far enough and the loop sustains itself — the filter keeps ringing forever at its cutoff frequency, with no input at all. It has become a sine-wave oscillator you steer with the cutoff knob. (In real hardware the tiniest circuit noise starts it — our worklet adds a whisper of noise for the same reason.)

This is why resonance and pitch are secretly the same phenomenon, and why the acid scream is the filter almost oscillating — a razor-edge between "emphasis" and "tone". The stock 303 is tuned to sit just below that line; push our RESONANCE past the red mark and you cross it, Devil-Fish style.

// CS corner — the ladder, in ~8 lines of per-sample DSP: 
// 4 one-pole low-passes in series, + feedback from the last one
u = tanh(x + noise − k·z4);        // k = resonance; tanh = analog saturation (bounds self-osc)
for each of 4 stages:  y = onepole(y);   // each adds a 45° phase shift…
z4 = y;  out = y;                  // …4×45° = 180° → the feedback flips sign → it oscillates
Four poles give exactly 180° of phase shift at the cutoff, so the fed-back signal reinforces itself → at k≈4 the loop gain hits 1 and it sings. BiquadFilterNode can't express this (no per-sample feedback you control), so it needs an AudioWorklet: your own DSP running on the audio thread, 128 samples at a time — the P3 for-loop and the lesson-10 blocks, made real.

soAnalog truth vs digital convenience

Everything you built was a model of this: OscillatorNode ≈ the capacitor ramp (minus the drift), the two biquads ≈ the diode ladder (minus the grit and the self-oscillation), the envelopes ≈ the analog CV circuits. Models are the right call — they're clean, cheap, and 90% of the sound. But now you've seen the other 10%: the electronics that make analog gear drift, dirty, and alive, and how to reach for a worklet when you want it back. You understand the 303 all the way down to the transistors.
