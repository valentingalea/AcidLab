♪ · Notes vs tone — what "playing" actually does

"I get the oscillator and the filter… but what does playing a note even do? If I have 16 A's and put a G in the middle — what is that?" Great question. Here's the whole answer, and you can hear it.

Tap any bar to change that step's note (it cycles up the scale). Or hit the two presets — that's your exact question. The big number up top is the oscillator frequency; watch it as it plays.

Now drag TONE while it loops: the tune is identical — same notes, same order — only the colour changes. Notes and tone are two separate dials.

idea 1A note is just a frequency — nothing more

Here's the whole secret, and it's smaller than you think. Back in P1 you learned pitch = how fast the air wiggles. A musical note is simply a named wiggle-speed:

notereally means
A (A2)"vibrate 110 times a second"
G (G3)"vibrate 196 times a second"
C, D, E……every note is just a different number of wiggles/sec

So "playing a note" = setting the oscillator's frequency knob to that number. That's it. You already met the knob in lesson 00 (the frequency slider) and again in lesson 05 (the keyboard) — every key just parks the oscillator on a different frequency. There is no separate "note machine"; the note is the frequency.

idea 2Your "16 A's and a G" — answered

So what is 16 A's with a G in the middle? It's a list of frequencies over time — a bassline. The oscillator's frequency knob sits on A (110 Hz) step after step, then for one step it jumps to G (196 Hz), then straight back to A. Hit 16 A's then put a G in the middle above and listen: fifteen identical thumps, then one that pops out — and suddenly it's a little tune instead of a machine gun.

That's not a beginner mistake — it's a real acid technique. Classic 303 basslines are mostly the root note hammered over and over, with the occasional other note (a passing G, an octave jump, a chromatic step) poked in to give it shape and pull. You just invented an acid bassline by changing one number, once.

Playing music, at the bottom, is nothing but choosing which frequency the oscillator should be at each moment. A melody is a sequence of those choices. That's all a "note" ever was.

idea 3Two separate dials: tune and tone

This is the part that untangles everything. There are two completely independent things going on:

controlled bydecides
the tunethe notes (oscillator frequency, step by step)which pitches — the melody / bassline
the tonethe filter, envelope, resonance, drivehow each note sounds — bright, dark, plucky, dirty

They don't touch each other. The filter does the exact same thing to an A as it does to a G — it carves and shapes whatever frequency happens to be playing. Proof: with the loop running, drag TONE above — the notes never change, only the colour does. And flipping notes in the lane never changes the tone. Everything you built in lessons 02–09 was the tone half; the notes are the other half, and this is where they meet.

That's why the same three-note riff can sound like a warm dub bass or a screaming acid line: same tune, different tone.

idea 4"…is this the sequencer?"

Yes and no — good instinct. The sequencer (coming in lesson 13) is just the tool for writing down that list of notes: a 16-step grid where you type a pitch per step, plus the accent (lesson 07) and slide (lesson 08) switches. But the idea — a note is a frequency, a bassline is a sequence of frequencies fed to the oscillator one step at a time on the clock (lesson 10) — is exactly what you're doing right here. Every "acid line" button since lesson 06 was a little sequencer playing a list like this one.

// CS corner: the pattern is literally an array of MIDI note numbers, e.g. [57,57,57,57, 57,57,57,57, 57,57,55,57, …] (57 = A, 55 = G). Each clock step the scheduler does osc.frequency.setValueAtTime(mtof(pattern[i]), when) where mtof(m)=440·2^((m−69)/12) — the one formula from P1. The filter graph never changes; only that one number is rewritten each step. Melody = writing a frequency; timbre = everything downstream of it.

Now nothing about the 303 is mysterious: the notes pick the pitches, the voice (osc → filter → env → drive) colours them, and the clock keeps time. That's the whole machine.
