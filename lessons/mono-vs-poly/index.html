♬ · Mono vs poly — how a synth plays a chord

The 303 plays one note at a time. So how does a synth play a whole chord? It has several copies of the voice — and a traffic cop. Watch it happen.

Power on, set VOICES, then play a chord — watch which voices light up.
play a chord:

Set VOICES to 1 and play A minor: you hear just one note — the three notes fight over the single voice and the last one wins (a steal, flashed red). Now drag VOICES up to 3–4 and play it again: each note gets its own voice and the chord actually rings. Then try the 6-note cluster on 4 voices to watch stealing.

idea 1One voice = one note. Always.

A "voice" is one full signal path: oscillator → filter → amp — the exact thing you built for the 303. It has a single oscillator, and an oscillator can only be at one frequency at a time. So one voice can only ever sound one note. Ask it to play a three-note chord and it can't — each new note just steals the oscillator, resetting it to the new pitch. The chord collapses to whichever note landed last.

That's not a flaw, it's a definition: a monophonic synth is a one-voice synth. The 303, the Minimoog, the SH-101 — all mono. You heard this back in lesson 05 when a second key "stole" the pitch. It's why acid is a single snaking bassline and never a chord.

idea 2Polyphony = several voices + a traffic cop

To play a chord you need to sound several notes at once — so a polyphonic synth simply contains several complete copies of the voice. A "4-voice" synth is literally four mini-303s in a box. When you press a chord, a bit of logic called the voice allocator hands each note to a free copy: note 1 → voice 1, note 2 → voice 2, note 3 → voice 3. Now three oscillators are running three pitches, and you hear a chord.

Drag VOICES from 1 upward while replaying A minor and you can literally hear the chord fill in, one note at a time, as more copies of the voice become available. Each lit box in the rack is one working voice holding one note — you're watching the allocator do its job.

// CS corner: a voice pool with an allocator — you basically wrote one in the scheduler already.
noteOn(n){ const v = pool.find(free) ?? stealOldest(); v.play(n); }
Each voice is the mono render(note, patch) instanced N times: they share the patch (one global tone — the point from the last lesson still holds) but each keeps its own envelope phase, filter state, and current note. That per-voice state — not per-voice settings — is what lets one note swell while another decays.

idea 3Voice stealing — when you run out

Real synths have a fixed number of voices (5, 6, 8, 16…). Play more notes than that — a big cluster, or fast notes that pile up before old ones fade — and the allocator has to steal: it kills a voice that's already sounding to free it up. Which one? Usually the oldest note (it's had its moment) or the quietest (least missed). Do it clumsily and you get audible notes cutting off; do it well and nobody notices.

Play the cluster (6 notes) with VOICES at 4: two notes have nowhere to go, so the two oldest get stolen (watch them flash red). This is the daily reality of every hardware poly synth — and why "how many voices?" was the headline spec people paid thousands for.

idea 4Why it was hard — and where "per-note tone" really lives

Now the history makes sense. An 8-voice analog synth meant eight of everything — eight oscillators, eight filters, eight envelope generators — plus a computer to run the allocator. That's why the early polys (Yamaha CS-80, Prophet-5, Jupiter-8) were heavy, temperamental, and cost a fortune. The Prophet-5 (1978) was the breakthrough: a microprocessor did the voice allocation and remembered your patch, and the mono era was basically over.

And your instinct from last lesson holds up: the voices all share one patch — one global tone. You don't dial a different sound per note. Where notes do differ in tone, it comes from per-note inputs layered on that shared patch:

per-note inputdoes
velocityhit harder → the patch maps it to louder/brighter
aftertouchpress harder after → vibrato, opens the filter
key trackinghigher notes automatically brighter
MPE (modern)per-note bend/pressure/slide — a ROLI Seaboard, LinnStrument

So: poly = N copies of the voice sharing one tone, each with its own envelope; real per-note expression is a separate layer on top. And the 303? Gloriously, defiantly one voice — which is exactly why it does what it does.
