12 · The drum machine

The synth drums (lesson 11) + the rock-solid clock (lesson 10) + a grid you can draw on. This little interface built house, techno and hip-hop.

patterns:

Tap cells to place hits. Tap a drum name to mute its row. Load a preset, hit ▶ play, then draw over it while it loops. Nudge swing up for groove.

idea 1A grid of time

Read the grid like a spreadsheet of a single bar. Left→right is time — 16 steps, the 16th-note grid from lesson 10 (four beats, four steps each; the darker columns are the beats 1·2·3·4). Top→bottom is the kit — one row per drum. A lit cell means "fire this drum on this step." The blue playhead sweeps left to right and loops forever, triggering whatever's lit as it passes.

That's the entire idea, and it's genuinely how a TR-808 or TR-909 works. Before this, programming a rhythm meant playing it in real time or splicing tape. Turning the beat into a grid you draw on — visual, editable, loopable — is one of the most consequential interfaces in music.

idea 2Building a beat is a few rules of thumb

You already have everything to make real grooves. The classic moves:

put…on steps…and you get
kick1, 5, 9, 13 (every beat)"four-on-the-floor" — the house/techno pulse
snare / clap5 and 13 (beats 2 & 4)the backbeat — the "off" that makes you nod
closed hatsthe in-betweens (8ths or 16ths)the shimmer that fills the gaps and drives it forward
open hatthe off-beats (3, 7, 11, 15)the pumping "tss" between kicks — pure house

Load house, techno and breaks and compare how the cells sit — then move things one step and hear the feel change. Kick landing a step early or late completely changes the groove; that's the whole craft.

idea 3Swing — why a machine can still groove

A perfectly-quantised beat can feel stiff. Swing (a.k.a. shuffle) fixes that by nudging every other 16th step slightly late — so the grid stops being perfectly even and starts to lope. It's the difference between a drum machine sounding robotic and sounding like it's playing. Push the swing knob up with hats running and feel it start to shuffle.

// CS corner: swing is a scheduling tweak, not a data change. The pattern grid stays a clean 16-step array; when the scheduler places step s, it just adds a delay to the odd ones: when = stepTime + (s % 2 ? swing · stepDur : 0). Everything else — the pattern (a {drum: bool[16]} map), the per-step loop firing lesson-11 voices, the lookahead from lesson 10 — is exactly what you've already built. The whole machine is ~20 lines around code you wrote.

idea 4Half a groovebox

Stand back: you now have a real, programmable drum machine — synthesised voices, a step grid, solid timing, swing — built entirely from parts you understand. Pair it with the 303 voice (lessons 05–09) and you're one step from the capstone: a groovebox where a drum pattern and an acid bassline lock to the same clock. That's the sequencer & DS-10-style build coming next — the drums are half of it, and they're done.
