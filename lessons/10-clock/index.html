idea 1Music is unforgiving about time

Your ear can hear a note that's a few milliseconds out of place. A drummer who wobbles by 20 ms sounds drunk; by 50 ms, broken. So a sequencer's single most important job is to fire notes at exactly the right instant, over and over, for hours, without ever drifting or flinching.

You'd think that's easy — "play a note every 117 ms" — and you'd write setInterval(playNote, 117). It even sounds fine… right up until the computer gets busy. Then it falls apart, and understanding why is the whole lesson.

idea 2Why setTimeout can't keep time

The trap: setTimeout and setInterval run your callback on the main thread — the same single thread doing layout, painting, garbage collection, and every other bit of JavaScript on the page. When that thread is busy, your "play now" callback simply waits its turn. The note fires late — whenever the thread gets around to it.

Worse, the errors accumulate: setTimeout(fn, 117) means "at least 117 ms", never exactly, and each late start pushes the next one later still. Try it: with setTimeout selected, hit 💣 stress the CPU and watch the bars scatter and the rhythm stumble. That's not a bug in your code — it's the wrong clock.

idea 3Two clocks — and one of them is perfect

Here's the key realisation. There are two different clocks in the browser:

JavaScript — setTimeout, Date.now()the busy main threadsloppy, jittery, drifts

And crucially: Web Audio lets you schedule a sound in the future. Every start and every parameter ramp takes a when: osc.start(when), gain.setValueAtTime(v, when) — you've used this in every lesson. That when is honoured on the audio thread with sample precision, no matter what the main thread is doing. So the plan writes itself: stop trying to play notes "now", and instead tell the audio clock exactly when to play them, ahead of time.

idea 4The lookahead scheduler (the fix)

The pattern is "A Tale of Two Clocks", and it's what Tone.js, every DAW, and this course have used since lesson 03. A lazy, imprecise timer wakes up every so often and, each time, schedules every note that falls in the next little window onto the precise audio clock:

const LOOKAHEAD = 0.1;    // schedule 100 ms into the future
let nextNoteTime = ctx.currentTime;
setInterval(() => {                // a sloppy timer — its jitter doesn't matter
  while (nextNoteTime < ctx.currentTime + LOOKAHEAD) {
    playClick(nextNoteTime);        // ← scheduled at an EXACT audio-clock time
    nextNoteTime += beatDur;        // advance the grid: 60/BPM/4 for 16ths
  }
}, 25);                          // wake ~40×/sec; only needs to beat LOOKAHEAD

The trick is a division of labour: the sloppy setInterval only decides when to think about scheduling — if it fires late, it just catches up on the next wake-up. It never decides when a sound actually plays; the audio clock does, precisely. As long as the timer wakes up more often than the lookahead window (100 ms) is long, notes are always queued before they're due. Select lookahead scheduler above, hit 💣 stress the CPU, and the bars stay flat. The main thread is on fire and the beat doesn't care.

// CS corner: two independent time bases. ctx.currentTime advances on the audio render thread (128-sample blocks, from lesson P3); your JS reads it but can't stall it. The scheduler is a producer filling a queue ~100 ms ahead of a consumer that drains it in real time — a classic lock-free-ish double-buffer. Tune two numbers: the interval (how often you wake) and the lookahead (how far you queue). Bigger lookahead = more jank-proof but laggier response to tempo changes; that trade-off is why a live knob feels a beat behind on some software.

soYou now have a transport

That's BPM → beat duration → a steady grid of instants, immune to whatever the page is doing. It's the heartbeat every sequencer needs.
