idea 1Your hand, but automatic

In lesson 03 you made the acid squelch by sweeping the cutoff by hand. In lesson 05 the filter just sat still. This lesson connects the two: a filter envelope — a second copy of the ADSR from lesson 01, wired to the cutoff instead of the volume — that does the sweep for you, freshly on every note.

The 303 gives that envelope a fixed instant attack and just two knobs, and they're the two most important knobs in acid:

knobwhat it setsear
Env Modhow far above the cutoff floor each note kicks the filter open (the sweep's height)0 = dead/static · high = a big wide-open wow
Decayhow fast the filter falls back down to the floor after the kickshort = a tight percussive blip · long = a slow talking wah

Watch the left graph: it's the cutoff over time for one note — flat at the floor, a vertical snap up to the peak, then a decay back down. Env Mod is the height of that snap; Decay is the width of the slope. Press ▶ acid line and watch the orange cutoff marker on the spectrum jump and slide on every note.

idea 2Env Mod is the depth of the sweep

The CUTOFF knob is now a floor — where the filter rests between the peaks. Env Mod stacks the envelope on top of it: each note snaps up to floor × 2^(envmod) octaves, then decays back to the floor.

  • Env Mod = 0: no kick — you're back to lesson 05, every note the same dull brightness.
  • A little: a gentle per-note bloom, subtle movement.
  • A lot: each note flings the filter wide open then slams shut — the full acid wowww.

Set the floor low (dark, so there's lots of room above it) and give Env Mod plenty of travel — that low-floor-plus-big-sweep is the classic dark acid bass. If the floor is already high, the sweep has nowhere to go and the effect vanishes.

idea 3Decay is the length of the "wow"

Decay is the single knob a 303 player rides most. It sets how long the filter takes to fall from the peak back to the floor:

  • Short (~50–150 ms): a sharp blip / pew — the filter's already shut by the time your ear catches up. Punchy, percussive, techno.
  • Medium (~200–400 ms): the classic talking wow — you clearly hear it open and close.
  • Long (~600 ms+): a slow wahhh that can outlast the note, so the filter is still gliding as the next one hits — liquid and overlapping.

With the ▶ acid line running, sweep Decay slowly from left to right. That change — nothing else — is most of what a 303 performance is: one hand riding Decay (and cutoff) while the pattern loops.

// CS corner: the filter env is pure AudioParam automation on the biquads' frequency, retriggered per note: setValueAtTime(floor) → linearRampToValueAtTime(peak, +3ms) → exponentialRampToValueAtTime(floor, +decay). Exponential (not linear) decay is why it sounds natural — like the analog capacitor it models discharging. Both cascaded biquads get the same automation so the 24 dB slope moves as one.

idea 4Env Mod × Resonance = the scream

Now bring back RESONANCE from lesson 03. The envelope sweeps the cutoff, and the resonant peak rides along with it — so on every note the emphasis whistle streaks up through the harmonics and back down. Env Mod provides the motion; resonance provides the voice. Together they're the entire acid scream, and now it's automatic and in time, one burst per note.

That's the 303 voice essentially complete: oscillator → 24 dB resonant filter → amp, with an envelope on the cutoff. What's left are the two per-note performance tricks — Accent (lesson 07: make some notes louder, brighter, snappier) and Slide (lesson 08: glide the pitch between notes) — the expression on top of the sound you just built.

Experiments:

  • Textbook acid: saw, floor low, resonance ~14, Env Mod ~75%, Decay ~250 ms — run the acid line.
  • Techno blip: same but Decay right down to ~60 ms — dry and percussive.
  • Liquid: Decay up past 600 ms — the wows smear into each other.
  • Kill it: pull Env Mod to 0 — instantly back to the flat lesson-05 tone. That gap is the lesson.
