DS-10 · The whole studio that hid inside a toy

The machine this course has aimed at the whole time. Here's where it came from — and its one trick you haven't built yet: the Kaoss pad, a touch surface that rides two knobs with one finger.

play itThe Kaoss pad — two knobs, one finger

Power on, hit play, then drag on the black pad. In ride mode your finger is two knobs at once: left↔right is cutoff, up↕down is resonance — the whole squelch surface under one thumb. Switch to play and the pad becomes an instrument: left↔right picks the note, up↕down opens the brightness.

This is the Nintendo DS's touchscreen doing exactly what it did in 2008. Remember DJ Pierre in the 303 story, riding one filter knob with his hand? The Kaoss pad lets you ride two at once.

1978 · the other synthKorg's answer: the MS-10

While Roland was building sealed little sequenced boxes, Korg (a Tokyo company founded in 1963) took the opposite bet with the MS series in 1978. The MS-10 was a cheap, hands-on monophonic synth — and by now you know its whole guts:

  • one oscillator (lesson 00),
  • one resonant low-pass filter — Korg's own gentler 12 dB/oct design (lessons 02–03),
  • one envelope (lesson 01),
  • and — borrowed from its bigger brother the MS-20 — a patch panel: real cables you plug to route anything into anything.

That patchbay is the key difference in philosophy. The 303 hid everything behind a sequencer and asked you to program notes blind. The MS put the synth in your hands — knobs and cables, no sequencer at all. One machine to compose with, one machine to play on. The DS-10 is the second kind.

// heads-up: the DS-10 is named for the MS range but isn't a strict MS-10 — each of its two synths actually has two oscillators, where the real MS-10 had one. We'll pull the DS-10 and the 303 apart knob-by-knob in the next lesson.

2008 · a synth in a games consoleThe idea nobody expected

Fast-forward thirty years. Nobuyoshi Sano — a game composer (the Ridge Racer and Tekken soundtracks) whose first synth was an MS-10 — was fiddling with a Nintendo DS and noticed something: the little clamshell console looked like an MS synth. Two panels hinged together. And that bottom touchscreen was begging to be a Kaoss pad.

What if the synth wasn't a game about music — but an actual playable Korg you could carry in your pocket?

Korg blessed it, the studio Cavia built it, and AQ Interactive shipped KORG DS-10 in 2008 for about the price of a game. For ~$40 you got two synths, a drum machine, a sequencer, and — the party trick — up to eight consoles jamming together over the DS's wireless link. A generation of chiptune and bedroom producers cut their teeth on it; Sano's team went on to found Detune and keep making Korg pocket synths. This is the machine our whole course has been quietly rebuilding.

the padWhat a Kaoss pad actually is

The Kaoss Pad is a real Korg product line from 1999: a flat X/Y touch surface. Where a knob controls one thing, the pad reads two numbers from one finger — how far across (X) and how far up (Y) — and maps each to a parameter. Move diagonally and you're turning two knobs in a single gesture. The Nintendo DS already had the perfect hardware for it sitting right there: a touchscreen.

On the DS-10 that pad wears two hats — the two modes in the toy above:

  • Ride — assign any two synth parameters to X and Y and perform them live. The classic pairing is cutoff × resonance: the entire "acid squelch" laid out as a 2-D surface you smear your thumb across.
  • Play — the pad becomes a fretless keyboard. Left-to-right is pitch (locked to a scale so it can't sound wrong), and it glides between notes — the same slide you built in lesson 08.
// CS corner: the pad is just two floats. A touch at pixel (px,py) becomes x = px/width, y = 1 − py/height — both in [0,1]. In ride mode we map x to cutoff on a log curve (C_MIN·(C_MAX/C_MIN)^x, so equal finger-distance = equal musical steps) and y straight to filter Q. In play mode x is quantised to a scale index floor(x·N). That's the entire Kaoss trick: normalise, then assign.
