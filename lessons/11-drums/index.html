idea 1A drum is a synth voice that dies fast

You might assume drum machines play little recordings. The classics — the Roland TR-808 and TR-909 that built house, techno and hip-hop — don't. Every sound is synthesised on the fly from the exact primitives you've been using: oscillators, noise, filters, and envelopes. A drum is just a very short, very percussive synth note: it slams on and decays in a fraction of a second. That's the whole trick — fast envelopes.

Two ingredients cover an entire kit: a tone (a pitched oscillator) for anything with body — kicks, toms, the "thud" of a snare — and noise (random samples, from lesson P3) for anything hissy — hats, the "snap" of a snare, claps. Mix and shape those two with envelopes and you have drums.

idea 2The kick — a sine that falls

The kick is the most elegant. It's a single sine wave (deep, pure, no harshness) with two envelopes firing at once:

  • a pitch envelope — the sine starts high (~150 Hz) and drops to its base pitch (~50 Hz) in about 50 ms. That fast downward swoop is the "thump" / punch of the attack.
  • an amp envelope — full volume instantly, then a decay to silence (the kick decay knob). Long = a booming 808 sub; short = a tight 909 thud.

That's it — a sine, a pitch drop, a volume drop. Turn kick tune down and kick decay up for a chest-caving 808; tune up and decay down for a punchy techno kick. On the scope you can literally see the wave slow down (the pitch falling) as it fades.

idea 3Noise makes the rest — snare, hats, clap

Everything hissy is filtered noise plus an envelope. Same recipe, different filter and decay:

drumrecipe
Snarea tone body (two triangle waves ~180 Hz — the "thud") + noise through a high-pass (the "snap"). The snare snap knob sets how much noise vs body.
Closed hatbright noise (high-pass, hat brightness) with a tiny decay (~40 ms). A short "tss".
Open hatthe same noise, but a long decay (~300 ms) — a sizzling "tssssss". Closed and open are one sound, two envelope lengths.
Clapthe clever one: 3–4 quick noise bursts ~12 ms apart (imitating many hands not quite in sync), then a short noise tail. That stutter is what makes a clap sound like a clap and not a snare.

Hit each pad and watch the scope: the kick is a smooth low wave, the hats are a dense high-frequency fuzz, the snare is a fuzz riding on a tone, the clap is those little repeated bursts.

idea 4Put it on the clock

A kit is nothing without time — so the ▶ demo beat hangs these voices on the lookahead scheduler from lesson 10: kick on every beat (four-on-the-floor), a snare backbeat, hats on the off-beats, a clap. Rock-solid timing, entirely synthesised, zero samples. Tweak the drum knobs while it plays and reshape the kit live.

This is the other half of a groovebox. You have the melodic voice (the 303, lessons 05–09) and now the rhythm (a synth drum kit). Next we give the drums a 16-step grid so you can program your own beats — the drum machine.
