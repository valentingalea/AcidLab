02 · Low-pass filter & cutoff

Start too bright, then carve the top away. One knob on this page — it's the knob that is acid.

idea 1You already know this sound — it's the "next room" effect

Music behind a closed door. A party heard from the street. Someone talking with a hand over their mouth. In every case the sound gets muffled: the boomy lows survive, the crisp highs die. Walls are a filter — they let low wiggles pass and block fast ones. That's exactly what a low-pass filter is, as a circuit (or 5 lines of code) instead of a wall.

Try it: ⏻ power on → ▶ hold note → drag the big orange CUTOFF slider all the way down and back up. You just walked in and out of the party. Now click filter: ON to bypass it — that's the raw, unfiltered saw from lesson 00.

idea 2Subtractive synthesis — sculpt by removing

From P1 and lesson 00: a saw wave is a stack of harmonics — the note you hear plus overtones at 2×, 3×, 4×… all the way up. The spectrum on the right shows the stack as green spikes.

A synth works like a sculptor: start with a block that has too much, and carve. The saw is the marble block (every harmonic present); the filter is the chisel; the cutoff sets where you start carving. Watch the spectrum while you drag: spikes above the orange line vanish, right to left. The shape on the left rounds off too — remove the fast wiggles from a jagged wave and only the smooth swing remains.

Compare the blocks: the square has only the odd harmonics (hollower — and its low end survives filtering as a fat sub-bass), and noise is every frequency at once — filtered noise stops being "shhh" and becomes wind, surf, distant rain. All three respond to the same knob.

idea 3Cutoff isn't a wall — it's a slope

The orange curve on the spectrum is the filter's real answer to "how much of each frequency do you let through?" (drawn live from the actual filter). Notice it doesn't drop like a cliff at the cutoff — it rolls off, a ski slope, not a wall. Harmonics just above the cutoff are quieter; ones far above are gone.

How steep that slope is becomes the sound-design decision: this single filter rolls off gently (12 dB per octave). The TB-303's famous filter is twice as steep (24 dB/oct — it stacks two of these), which is a big part of why it sounds so focused and rubbery. We build that in lesson 03, along with the filter's party trick: resonance, a boost right at the cutoff edge that this page keeps switched off.

// CS corner: the whole filter is BiquadFilterNode — a 5-multiply recurrence over the P3 sample array: y[n] = b0·x[n] + b1·x[n−1] + b2·x[n−2] − a1·y[n−1] − a2·y[n−2]. Feedback of past outputs is what makes it an IIR filter — each sample remembers a decaying echo of everything before, and fast changes can't get through. "12 dB/oct" means: go up one octave (×2 frequency) past the cutoff and the filter outputs ~¼ the amplitude.

idea 4A still filter is tone control. A moving filter is acid

Parked in one spot, a low-pass filter is just the "treble" knob on an old stereo. The magic is motion: sweep the cutoff while a note plays and the sound goes wow → ooh → wow — the same trick your mouth does when you change its shape while humming. Press ∿ auto-sweep and just listen (works great on all three sources; the slider rides along).

Every classic acid line is this: a plucky note repeating while something turns the cutoff knob. In lesson 06 that "something" stops being your hand and becomes an envelope — the ADSR from lesson 01 aimed at the filter instead of the volume. That's the squelch. You now know all the parts; what's left is wiring them together.

Experiments before you leave:

  • Ocean: source noise + auto-sweep — instant waves on a beach.
  • Dub bass: source square, cutoff down to ~200 Hz — a warm sub with no edge.
  • Radio voice: saw, cutoff ~800 Hz — thin and boxy, like a cheap speaker.
  • The carve: saw, drag slowly from open to closed and try to hear individual spikes drop off the top.
