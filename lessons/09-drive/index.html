09 · Drive / saturation — the dirty edge

The 303 has no distortion knob. So how is hard acid so filthy? You overdrive it. Same trick you feared in lesson P3 — now weaponised.

idea 1Distortion is just clipping, on purpose

Back in lesson P3, when a signal got too loud and slammed into the ±1 ceiling, its peaks got chopped flat and it sounded nasty — clipping, the thing you were trying to avoid. Distortion is deliberately doing exactly that. You shove the signal too hard into a limit, the peaks flatten, and flattening a smooth wave adds new harmonics — that extra fizz and grind is the "dirt".

A pure saw is already buzzy; squash its tips and you pile even more high harmonics on top — the sound gets thicker, louder, more aggressive, and it bites. That's saturation. It's the same physics as a tube amp being cranked, a fuzz pedal, or a mixing desk pushed into the red.

idea 2The transfer curve — a shape that reshapes

The tool is a wave shaper: a lookup curve that maps every input sample to an output sample. The left graph is that curve — input along the bottom, output up the side. A straight diagonal (clean) means "out = in", no change. Bend that line and you bend the sound:

  • where the curve is steep, the signal gets louder;
  • where it flattens off near the top, loud peaks get squashed together — that flat shoulder is the clipping.

The DRIVE knob is a pre-amp: it scales the signal up before the curve, so more of the wave is shoved out onto that flat shoulder. Low drive tickles the middle (clean-ish); high drive pins the whole wave to the clipped ends. Watch the green dots on the curve — they're the actual signal, crawling out toward the corners as you drive harder.

idea 3Three flavours of dirt

Same idea, different curve shapes — each a different character:

shapecurvesound
soft (tube)a gentle S that eases into the limitwarm, fat, musical — thickens without getting harsh. The everyday "make it beefier".
hard (fuzz)a sharp corner then dead flatbuzzy, raspy, aggressive — classic distortion-pedal grind. Hard acid lives here.
fold (metal)the curve turns back on itselfwild, clangorous, inharmonic — the peak doesn't just clip, it folds over. Ring-mod-ish, brutal.

Flip between them with the line running and the same notes go from warm to nasty to unhinged. Soft is the safe workhorse; fold is a weapon — use sparingly.

idea 4Why acid needs it — drive × resonance

Here's the thing the classic records know: a resonant filter and distortion are a vicious team. The filter's resonant peak makes one loud, narrow whistle; drive it, and that whistle turns into a scream — the harmonics pile up right where the ear is already focused. Turn resonance up and DRIVE up together and the squelch stops being cute and starts being menacing. That's the sound of acid techno, of Hardfloor, of Josh Wink's "Higher State".

The 303 famously has no distortion of its own — so producers ran it into whatever they had: a cranked mixer channel, a guitar pedal, a broken preamp. The dirt is external, and half the craft of acid is where and how hard you add it. You just built that stage.

Experiments:

  • Beef: soft, DRIVE ~8×, output down a touch — a fatter bass, same notes.
  • The scream: resonance ~20, hard, DRIVE high — the squelch bares its teeth.
  • Broken: fold, mid drive — melodic notes turn into clanging metal.
  • A/B honestly: tap clean then soft — hear how much "weight" a little saturation adds.

The 303 voice is complete and filthy. From here it's the machine around it: the clock (lesson 10), drums, and the sequencer.
