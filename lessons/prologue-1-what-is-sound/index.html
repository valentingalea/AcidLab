Section 1Sound is air wiggling

A speaker is a little drum skin that pushes air, pulls it back, pushes again. Each push shoves a ripple of squeezed air across the room into your ear. That's all sound is — air wiggling back and forth.

Here's the fun part. If the speaker pushes just a couple of times a second, you hear each push as a separate click. Push faster and the clicks blur into a flutter. Push faster still and — listen — they melt into a note:

A "note" and "clicks repeating fast" are the same thing. Around 20–30 wiggles per second your ear stops counting pushes and starts hearing pitch. Everything else in music theory is built on this one fact.

Section 2Faster wiggle = higher sound

How fast the air wiggles is called frequency, counted in wiggles-per-second = Hertz (Hz). Fast wiggle → high, whistly sound. Slow wiggle → deep, bassy sound. That's the whole definition of pitch. Drag the slider while the tone plays:

440 Hz

Human ears hear roughly 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. A bass drum lives near the bottom, a mosquito near the top. The slider feels "even" because it's actually exponential — more on that in section 4.

Section 3Bigger wiggle = louder sound

The speed of the wiggle was pitch. The size of the wiggle — how hard the air gets pushed — is loudness (the technical word is amplitude). Same note, three sizes:

Notice the pitch didn't change at all — speed and size are independent knobs. In a synth, the thing that controls moment-to-moment loudness is called an amplifier (VCA), and shaping it over time is lesson 01.

Section 4The magic doubling trick — the octave

Play a note. Now play the wiggle at exactly double speed. It doesn't sound like a random higher note — it sounds like the same note, just higher. Every human culture noticed this. The distance from a note to its double is called an octave:

This is why pitch works like a ladder that repeats: go up one octave and you're "back at the same rung", one floor higher.

// CS corner: your ear hears ratios, not differences. 110→220 and 440→880 feel like the same jump even though one is +110 Hz and the other +440 Hz. Pitch is logarithmic — which is why the slider in section 2 maps position to freq = 50 · 40^(x/100) instead of a straight line.

Section 5Cutting the ladder into 12 steps

Between a note and its double there are infinitely many frequencies. To build instruments (and play together) people agreed to pick a few fixed steps in each octave. The Western deal: cut every octave into 12 equal steps. One step is called a semitone — it's the distance between any two neighbouring keys on a piano. Hear all 12 steps climb one octave:

// CS corner: "12 equal steps that multiply to ×2" means each step multiplies frequency by 2^(1/12) ≈ 1.0595 — about 6% faster per step. This scheme is called equal temperament. The trade-off: except for the octave, every interval is a hair "impure" (irrational ratios), but in exchange the same 12 keys work in every key signature. Some Eastern traditions refused this deal and kept the notes between the keys — that's in P2.

Section 6The steps get names — the keyboard

The 12 steps have names: C C# D D# E F F# G G# A A# B — 7 letters (white keys) plus 5 "sharps" (black keys). After B the names wrap back to C, one octave up, forever. Play — click the keys, or use A W S E D F T G Y H U J K on your computer keyboard:

The reference point of all Western tuning: the A above middle C wiggles at 440 Hz.

// CS corner: since the steps are equal, every key can just be an integer. That numbering is MIDI: middle C = 60, A above it = 69, +12 = one octave. The entire keyboard is one formula: freq = 440 · 2^((midi − 69) / 12). Music, to a computer, is arrays of small ints — P2 runs with this.

Section 7One note is secretly many wiggles

Everything so far used a pure wiggle (a sine wave) — the smooth, whistle-like tones you've been hearing. But pluck a real guitar string and it doesn't wiggle in just one way: the whole string swings at some speed and at the same time its two halves swing at double speed, its thirds at triple speed, and so on. One pluck = a whole stack of wiggles at 1×, 2×, 3×, 4×… the base speed. These extras are called harmonics (or overtones).

Two payoffs, both huge:

payoffwhat it means
Timbre (tone colour)Why a violin and a flute playing the same note sound different: different recipe of harmonics. Synthesizers are machines for cooking harmonic recipes — you'll see the stack on a spectrum display in lesson 00.
HarmonyWalk up the stack again and listen: the 2× is the octave, and the 3× lands on a new, super-friendly note (the "fifth"). Notes sound good together when their stacks overlap. Which pairs of notes are friends — and how scales are built from them — is exactly what P2 is about.

Section 8Recap — the whole page in five lines

· Sound = air wiggling. Fast repeated clicks are a note.
· Wiggle speed = pitch (Hz) · wiggle size = loudness.
· Double the speed = octave = "same note, higher". Ears hear ratios.
· Each octave is cut into 12 equal steps (semitones) named C…B, numbered by MIDI.
· A real note is a stack of wiggles (harmonics) → gives timbre and harmony.
