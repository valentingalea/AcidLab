07 · Accent — the per-note punch

Same notes, but some of them hit. The one trick that turns a robotic loop into a groove.

Tap a step to accent / un-accent it (bar height = how hard the note hits). Accented steps are orange.

Start the loop, then drag ACCENT from 0 to full. At 0 every note is identical — a robot. Turn it up and the accented steps leap out. Then move the accents around and hear the groove change.

idea 1Not every note is equal

Play a drummer a steady stream of identical hits and it's a machine. Let a few land harder and suddenly there's a groove — your body finds the beat. Music lives in that unevenness. The 303's version of it is Accent: a single per-step switch that marks certain notes to punch.

You already heard this — the hands-free riff back in lesson 06 had accents baked in (that's why it grooved instead of marching). Now you get the knob. Set ACCENT to 0 above and listen: dead flat, every note the same, robotic. That flatness is what accent cures.

idea 2Accent does three things at once

Here's the clever bit. Flipping one switch doesn't just turn a note up — on a 303 an accented note is louder and brighter and snappier, all together. That combination is why an accented note doesn't just sound "up in volume", it sounds like it was hit harder — exactly like a real player digging in:

accent adds…howwhy it reads as "harder"
loudera boost to the amp (VCA)the obvious one — more volume
brighterextra kick to the filter envelope — the cutoff flies higher on that notea hard-plucked string is brighter; your ear reads brightness as force
snappiera faster filter decay on accented notesthe punch is quick and tight, not a slow swell

Watch the spectrum as the loop plays: on accented steps the whole curve leaps up and open, then snaps shut. The ACCENT knob scales all three at once — that's the single "amount" a 303 gives you.

idea 3Placement is the performance

Two controls, and between them they are the groove:

  • the ACCENT knob — how much extra punch (the amount, global);
  • the step grid — which notes get it (tap the steps).

Move the accents onto the downbeats (steps 1, 5, 9, 13) for a driving, four-on-the-floor push. Shove them off the beat for something syncopated and rolling. Accent the high notes to make them scream, or the low root to make it stomp. Same twelve notes, a dozen different feels — all from where the punches land. This is most of what a 303 player actually does with their hands: not changing notes, but changing emphasis.

// CS corner: accent is one boolean per step. On an accented step we scale three numbers by the accent amount a: amp gain ×(1+0.9a), filter-envelope peak ×(1+1.0a) (brighter), and filter decay ×(1−0.45a) (snappier). Un-accented notes stay at the base. One flag, three multiplies, live per step in the scheduler — that's the whole feature.

idea 4Accent × resonance = the acid bark

Turn resonance up and accent gets vicious: because accented notes fling the cutoff higher, the resonant peak streaks further up the harmonics on exactly those notes — so accents don't just punch, they bark and scream while the quiet notes stay dark and rubbery underneath. That contrast — a few screaming notes over a mumbling bassline — is the signature acid dynamic.

Experiments:

  • Feel the cure: loop running, sweep ACCENT 0 → 100% — robot → groove in one knob.
  • Downbeat drive: accent only steps 1 / 5 / 9 / 13 — a steady four-on-the-floor push.
  • Off-beat roll: accent only the off-beats — same notes, a rolling syncopated feel.
  • The bark: resonance ~18, accent ~90%, accent the octave jumps — those notes scream.

One trick left: Slide — gluing notes together with a pitch glide — and the 303 voice is complete.
