idea 1A synth voice is just modules in a row

Nothing new is invented in this lesson — you already built every piece. A synthesizer "voice" is just those pieces wired in order, each one feeding the next:

stagewhat it doesyou built it in
1 · Oscillatormakes the raw, harmonically-rich tone (saw or square)lesson 00
2 · Filterthe 24 dB low-pass with resonance — carves & colours the tonelessons 02–03
3 · Amplifier (VCA)an envelope shapes each note's loudness so it doesn't just dronelesson 01

That's the whole signal chain drawn above the keyboard. Sound flows left → right: a constant buzzy wave, sculpted by the filter, then switched on and off in shape by the amp. Press a key and watch the amp box light up — that's the envelope opening the gate.

idea 2Monophonic — one note at a time

A real 303 has one oscillator, so it can only play one note at a time — press a second key and it just steals the pitch of the first. Try it: hold one key, press another. That's not a limitation to apologise for — acid is a single snaking bassline, and monophony is why it sounds so focused and hypnotic. Melody, not chords.

Each key press re-fires the amp envelope — a fast attack then a decay set by the AMP DECAY knob. Short decay = a percussive blip (the classic 303 amp, which has essentially zero sustain); long decay = notes that ring into each other. This is exactly the ADSR from lesson 01, just permanently wired to the amplifier.

// CS corner: monophony is a one-slot voice allocator. We keep a stack of currently-held keys; noteOn pushes and steals the single oscillator's pitch, noteOff pops and hands the voice back to whatever key is still down (last-note priority). One OscillatorNode runs forever; we only ever change its frequency and re-trigger the amp gain — no per-note node churn.

idea 3Everything on this panel, you already understand

Five controls, zero mystery — each is a knob from an earlier lesson:

  • wave (saw / square) — the two the 303 offers. Saw = all harmonics (brighter), square = odd only (hollower, fatter lows). Lesson 00.
  • cutoff — how bright, where the filter starts carving. The soul knob. Lesson 02.
  • resonance — the emphasis/whistle at the cutoff edge. Lesson 03.
  • amp decay — how long each note rings. Lesson 01.
  • volume — overall level (with resonance make-up so high Q doesn't clip).

Set saw + cutoff low + a little resonance + short decay and play a low run — that's already a recognisable acid bass tone. Set square + high cutoff for a brighter lead. You have a real, playable subtractive synth.

idea 4It's a synth… but it isn't acid yet

Play a line and listen hard. It's a good bass, but every note has the same brightness — the filter just sits at whatever you set. The thing that makes acid talk is the cutoff moving on every single note, exactly like the ping and squelch in lesson 03 — but triggered automatically by each note instead of by your hand.

That's one wire away: point a second envelope (another ADSR, like lesson 01's) at the cutoff instead of the volume. That's the filter envelope — Env Mod and Decay — and it's the single biggest step toward the sound. It gets its own lesson next.

Experiments before you go:

  • Acid bass tone: saw, cutoff ~400 Hz, resonance ~12, decay ~200 ms — play a low A-root run.
  • Dub sub: square, cutoff right down, resonance 0, long decay — a round sub-bass with no edge.
  • Bright lead: saw, cutoff wide open, resonance ~6, play up high with X.
  • Blip vs drone: park cutoff + reso, then sweep AMP DECAY from 40 ms to max while repeating a note.
