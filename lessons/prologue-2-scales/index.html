Section 2Two notes at once — friends & enemies

Play two notes together and they either agree (smooth, calm) or argue (tense, buzzing). P1's harmonic stacks explain it: two notes agree when their stacks overlap a lot. The distance between two notes, counted in keys (semitones), is called an interval — and each distance has its own personality. Audition them all (each button = the two notes together):

The ones to remember:

intervalkeys apartpersonality
octave12same note, melts together (the ×2 from P1)

minor third3the sad one — the backbone of acid's dark mood
major third4the happy one

Section 3 ★Scales — the interactive index

You never use all 12 notes at once. A scale is a hand-picked subset — usually 5 to 7 notes — that share a mood. Stay on a scale's notes and almost anything you play sounds intentional; that's the whole trick. Pick a root (the "home" note) and a scale: the keyboard lights up its notes (orange = home, green = members) and plays it up and down. This page is your reference forever — steal freely.

"Its chord" = every other note of the scale stacked up — each scale carries its own chord flavour.

Section 4Modes — same notes, different home

Here's a mind-bender: take the 7 white keys. Treat C as home → bright, happy (major). Treat A as home → dark, sad (minor). Same seven notes. Nothing changed but which note feels like "home base" — and the whole mood flipped. Each choice of home within one note-set is called a mode. The seven modes of the white keys:

modeflavouryou know it from
Ionian (major)bright, resolvedmost pop, nursery rhymes

They're all in the index above — pick one root and audition the modes back to back to hear the mood shift. For acid you'll mostly live in natural minor and Phrygian, reaching for harmonic minor / Phrygian dominant when you want the exotic edge.

Section 5West vs East — the notes between the keys

Everything so far assumed the Western deal from P1: 12 equal steps. Other traditions divide the octave differently — and many use pitches that sit between our piano keys (microtones). Hear one: C, then a note halfway to C# (a "quarter tone" — no piano key exists for it), then C#:

Same 7-note "major-ish" scale, two worlds: Western major uses exact semitones; Arabic Maqam Rast flattens the 3rd and 7th note by a quarter tone into "neutral" notes that don't exist on any keyboard. Play them back to back — that half-flat is the whole flavour:

traditionthe ideasound / instrument
Western (12-TET)12 equal semitones; harmony via stacked chordspiano, most electronic music
Arabic / Turkish maqam~24 quarter-tones; melody + neutral thirds; no chordsoud, ney — the "Middle-Eastern" feel
Indian ragamelodic framework (not just a scale): ~22 shruti microtones, rules, mood, even a time of day; drone underneathsitar, tabla, tanpura drone
Japanese5-note scales (Hirajoshi, In) with dramatic half-stepskoto — also great for chiptune/game music
Indonesian gamelanslendro (5) & pelog (7), non-equal tunings unique to each ensemblemetallophones — "shimmering, out-of-tune" to Western ears

Acid is firmly Western 12-TET — but it borrows the flavour: the "exotic/eastern" vibe in dark acid & psytrance comes from Phrygian dominant / harmonic minor, which mimic maqam Hijaz using only the 12 keys. 80% of the mystery, none of the microtones.

Section 6Chords & rhythm (a peek — more later)

Chords = several notes at once. The basic unit is three notes: home + a third + a fifth. Whether that third is the sad one (3 keys) or the happy one (4 keys) decides everything:

Acid is mostly monophonic — one note at a time — so you'll rarely play chords; but a bassline implies a chord by outlining its notes.

Rhythm gets its own module (10–12); the vocabulary you need now: music is counted in bars of 4 beats (4/4 time). Split each beat into 4 → 16th notes: the grid of every 303 line and step sequencer. Speed is BPM (beats per minute); acid sits around 120–130.

Section 7The acid cheat-sheet

Remember nothing else but this and you'll make convincing acid lines:

  • Key: pick a root, use natural minor or Phrygian. A minor is the friendliest start (all white keys). For darker/eastern, Phrygian dominant or harmonic minor.
  • Safe notes: when in doubt, minor pentatonic (5 notes) — nearly impossible to hit a wrong note. Perfect for the game's auto-generated melodies.
  • Motion: acid lines lean on the root and octave, walk up/down by semitone (chromatic passing notes are welcome), and land back home. Repetition is the point.
  • Feel: 4/4, 16th-note grid, ~125 BPM. The groove comes from accents & slides (Module 2), not from lots of notes.
