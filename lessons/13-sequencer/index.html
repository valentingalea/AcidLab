idea 1Everything you built is on this one grid

Nothing here is new — it's the whole course wired together. Each of the 16 steps carries the four things a 303 pattern holds:

The playhead walks the 16 steps on the lookahead clock (lesson 10), and for each step it plays the 303 voice at that pitch, applying that step's accent and slide. That's a TB-303. You built every piece.

idea 2How to write an acid line

The cheat-sheet from the theory prologue, now actionable:

  • Lean on the root. Load acid 1 — mostly the low A, with the odd octave jump and a passing note. Acid lines are repetitive on purpose.
  • Rests are notes too. The gaps give it rhythm and bounce — a wall of 16 notes is a drone.
  • Accent a few steps (often the octave jumps or downbeats) so it grooves instead of marching.
  • Slide into a couple of notes — especially big jumps — for that liquid pour.
  • Then ride the knobs: sweep cutoff and env mod while it loops. That performance is acid — exactly what DJ Pierre discovered.

Not sure where to start? Hit 🎲 random acid line — it fills the grid with a scale-locked pattern (only notes from A minor pentatonic, so it can't sound wrong), then tweak from there.

idea 3A pattern is just data

// CS corner: the whole pattern is three little arrays — pitch[16] (0 = rest, else a scale index), accent[16], slide[16]. Each clock step the scheduler reads column s and drives the same monophonic voice you built: set osc.frequency (or ramp it, if the previous step had slide), fire the filter envelope (bigger + snappier if accented), shape the amp. The 🎲 button is a scale-constrained random walk — pick each note from the pentatonic mask, sprinkle accents and slides by probability. That exact idea — a random walk over a scale bitmask — is how we'll auto-compose music for a game in Module 6. Melody, in the end, is just data on a grid.

idea 4One clock away from the whole machine

You now have both halves running on the same kind of scheduler: this acid sequencer and the drum machine (lesson 12). Lock them to one shared clock, add a master mixer with the delay and reverb that glue acid together, and you have a groovebox — the capstone. The 303 is complete; from here it's assembly.
