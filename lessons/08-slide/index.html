08 · Slide / glide

The last trick: instead of jumping between notes, the pitch glides. The liquid that makes acid pour.

Tap a step to make it slide into the next (a → appears). On the graph: diagonal = a glide, vertical = a jump. Orange = accented.

Start the loop. The two blue steps already slide (octave glides) — hear how they pour into the next note instead of stepping. Tap more steps to add slides; drag SLIDE TIME to make the glide lazy or snappy.

idea 1Glide, don't jump

Every note so far has jumped straight to its pitch — the way a piano or fretted guitar works: one key, one exact pitch, no in-between. Slide (a.k.a. portamento or glide) does the opposite: the pitch slides continuously from one note to the next, sweeping through every pitch in between — like a finger dragging along a bass string, a trombone, or a singer swooping between words.

On the graph above, a slide is a diagonal ramp between two notes; a jump is a straight vertical line. Same two notes, completely different feel: the jump is articulate and rhythmic, the glide is liquid and vocal. Slide is a big part of why a 303 line sounds like it's flowing rather than stepping.

idea 2Slide glues two notes into one gesture

Here's the subtle, important part. When a note slides into the next, they're tied: the amp envelope does not re-fire on the second note — there's no fresh pluck, no gap. The sound just keeps going and the pitch bends underneath it. Two notes, but a single continuous gesture (musicians call this legato).

That tie is why slide feels so different from just "notes close together". Listen to the two blue steps: the octave jump doesn't re-attack — the low A pours smoothly up into the high A as one breath. Un-slid notes, by contrast, each get their own crisp pluck. The interplay of plucked notes and slid notes — punchy, then liquid, then punchy — is the acid line's phrasing.

// CS corner: two moves, both on the slid-into note. The pitch: osc.frequency.setValueAtTime(fromHz, t) then exponentialRampToValueAtTime(toHz, t+slideTime) — exponential because pitch is logarithmic, so an exponential Hz ramp is a straight, even glide in musical pitch. The tie: on that note we skip the amp attack and the filter-envelope retrigger, so the previous note's envelopes simply carry through. One flag per step decides both.

idea 3Where & how much

Two controls again:

  • the step grid — which notes slide into the next (tap them);
  • SLIDE TIME — how long the glide takes. The real 303 has one fixed, fairly quick glide (~60 ms); we made it a knob so you can hear the range, from an almost-jump to a long lazy swoop.

Slides shine on big intervals — an octave or a fifth glides dramatically — and on the note into a downbeat, so the beat arrives on a bend. Chain two or three slides in a row and the pitch just keeps pouring from note to note, never re-plucking: the signature liquid acid run. Try tapping steps to slide a whole phrase together.

idea 4That's the whole 303

Stop and look at what's on this page: an oscillator, a 24 dB resonant filter with an envelope, an amp, and a 16-step pattern where each step carries a pitch, an accent, and a slide. That is a complete TB-303 — every knob and switch, all built from scratch, all understood. The squelch, the punch, the liquid glide: you can make all of it now.

Experiments:

  • Feel the tie: loop running, tap step 3 on and off — hear it change from a crisp pluck to a glide into step 4.
  • Lazy vs snappy: slide a step, then sweep SLIDE TIME from 15 ms to 260 ms.
  • Liquid run: slide four steps in a row — the pitch never re-plucks, it just pours.
  • The full voice: resonance ~16, a couple of accents on downbeats, slides into them — that's an acid bassline.
